title = 'The Tyranny of "Good Enough"*'

I've been thinking about survivorship bias lately, and I've realized it operates at a level more fundamental than we typically acknowledge. We usually think of survivorship bias as a statistical problem—we only see the successful startups, the published authors, the survivors who made it through. But there's a deeper form operating within us, at the level of our biological systems. Our bodies and minds don't optimize for thriving; they optimize for not dying. This distinction matters profoundly because it means that many of the patterns we've developed—both as individuals and as a society—aren't designed to help us flourish. They're designed merely to keep us alive. When we mistake survival for validation, we end up perpetuating what I call "suboptimal solutions": ways of living and thinking that work just well enough to persist, but not well enough to let us truly thrive.

Consider how this works at the individual level. When you develop a coping mechanism for stress—perhaps you overwork, perhaps you withdraw from relationships, perhaps you develop rigid thinking patterns—your biological system runs a remarkably simple check: Are you still alive? If the answer is yes, the behavior continues. The system doesn't ask whether you're flourishing, building sustainable habits, or moving toward long-term wellbeing. It only registers that you survived another day, so whatever you're doing must be "good enough." This creates a dangerous feedback loop. Because the behavior doesn't produce immediate catastrophic consequences—because it doesn't literally kill you—your nervous system interprets it as safe, even functional. The lack of death becomes confused with proof of health. This explains why so many people persist in patterns that are clearly destructive in the long run. The chronic stress that will lead to burnout in five years doesn't kill you today. The relationship pattern that slowly erodes your capacity for intimacy doesn't kill you today. The anxiety that keeps you up at night, the perfectionism that drives away friends, the defensive behaviors that prevent genuine connection—none of these kill you immediately. Your nervous system, operating unconsciously and focused only on the present moment, knows just one thing: you're still here. And if you're still here, the behavior passes its only test. What doesn't kill you doesn't necessarily make you stronger. Sometimes it just makes you more tolerant of dysfunction, more adapted to circumstances that shouldn't be adapted to. Over time, we become so accustomed to operating in survival mode that we forget to ask whether we could be doing more than merely surviving.

What happens at the individual level—this confusion between "didn't kill me" and "good for me"—also happens at the collective level, shaping the wisdom we inherit and the systems of thought we take for granted. Just as our personal nervous systems privilege survival over thriving, cultures and societies pass down ideas that cleared the bar of "didn't destroy us" rather than "helped us flourish." The advice that gets passed down—"work hard and keep your head down," "don't trust people too easily," "success requires sacrifice"—wasn't tested against human flourishing. It was tested against survival. The people who lived by these principles survived to pass them on, and we interpret their survival as evidence of the principles' value. But we never hear from the alternative versions of those people who might have lived differently, who might have been more trusting or more balanced or more willing to challenge convention, because that experiment largely wasn't run, or when it was, those people didn't survive in the specific contexts that shaped our inherited wisdom. The same survivorship bias appears in career advice and self-help culture. Someone builds a successful company through grueling 80-hour weeks, writes a book about their methods, and suddenly that becomes the template others try to follow. We don't see the equally talented people who burned out attempting the same approach, who sacrificed their health or relationships in pursuit of the same goal but didn't make it to the finish line. They're not around to write books or give TED talks. Even in science, our supposedly rational and rigorous domain, paradigms persist not necessarily because they're true but because they're useful enough to survive academic and institutional pressures. We tell ourselves stories about progress and improvement, but often we're just watching ideas that were "good enough" outlast their competitors, not because they were optimal but because they cleared the remarkably low bar of not being fatal to the careers and reputations of those who held them.

Once I see this pattern operating at both the individual and collective levels, I can't unsee it, and it fundamentally changes how I evaluate the ideas and behaviors I've inherited. At the personal level, I need to question my own habits and coping mechanisms with new scrutiny: Is this pattern helping me thrive, or is it simply not killing me fast enough for my nervous system to sound the alarm? When I feel stressed but functional, exhausted but productive, anxious but managing—am I actually okay, or have I just become so adapted to dysfunction that I've lost the ability to recognize it? But the implications extend beyond self-examination. This realization unsettles me because it means I can't simply trust that established ideas are good—I can only trust that they were sufficient for survival in particular contexts. The wisdom of previous generations isn't wrong, exactly, but it's been filtered through survival rather than optimality. When I receive advice, inherit a worldview, or adopt a way of thinking, I need to ask not just "did this work for someone?" but "did this help them thrive, or did it merely help them not die?" The question isn't whether an idea or behavior has survived—many terrible things survive. The question is whether it moves us beyond mere survival toward something worth surviving for. And yet I'm aware of the irony here: even this skeptical framework I'm developing is itself just another idea that either will or won't survive in my own mind. If it helps me navigate the world, it'll persist. But that won't make it true or good—just good enough to keep me going. Perhaps the best I can do is remain conscious of this trap, to notice when I'm mistaking persistence for proof, survival for success. Perhaps the goal isn't to escape survivorship bias entirely—that may be impossible—but to raise the bar for what counts as "good enough," to ask not just whether something keeps me alive but whether it helps me live in a way that feels worth the effort of staying alive.
